# Salary Negotiation & Offer Evaluation — Senior Deep Reference

---

## The Mindset

```text
Negotiation is expected. Recruiters budget for it.
The offer you receive is not the offer — it's the opening bid.
You cannot lose an offer by negotiating professionally.
Silence and vagueness are tools. Use them.
```

---

## Before You Apply — Research Your Number

### Where to look

```text
levels.fyi       — best for total comp (base + bonus + equity) at tech companies
Glassdoor        — salary reports, interview experiences
LinkedIn Salary  — broad industry data
Blind            — candid peer discussion, especially for FAANG/big tech
Glassdoor + Blind together — triangulate, remove outliers
Recruiter calls  — ask "what is the band for this role?" early (they often share)
Your network     — the most accurate data you can get
```

### Know your number before any conversation

```text
Target number:   what you actually want (based on market research + your value)
Minimum floor:   what you will not go below (keep this private)
Walkaway point:  below this, you decline — know it clearly before you negotiate

Rule: your floor is your floor. If an offer is below it, decline.
      Never accept and plan to leave — it wastes everyone's time.
```

## During the Process — Information Control

### Never anchor first

```text
Recruiter: "What are your salary expectations?"

Bad:  "I'm looking for $150,000."  ← you've anchored, may have gone too low

Better: "I'm flexible — could you share the band for this role?
         I want to make sure we're aligned before going further."

If pushed hard:
  "Based on my research and experience, I'm targeting somewhere in the
   $X–$Y range, but I'm open depending on the full package."
  (Make the bottom of your range your actual target, not your floor)
```

### Never disclose your current salary (illegal to ask in many US states)

```text
Recruiter: "What are you making currently?"

Response: "I'd prefer to focus on the value I bring to this role and
           what the market rate is, rather than my current comp.
           Could you share the band?"

Or in states where they can't ask:
  "I understand that's not something companies in [state] can ask —
   happy to discuss what I'm targeting instead."
```

### Create urgency (competing offers)

```text
The single most powerful negotiation lever is a real competing offer.

"I'm currently in final rounds with two other companies and expect
 offers by [date]. I'm most excited about this role, but I need to
 make a decision by then."

This is honest only if true. Do not fabricate offers — it can backfire.
The right move: run multiple processes simultaneously, time them to converge.
```

## When You Receive an Offer

### Never accept on the spot

```text
Always say: "Thank you so much — I'm really excited about this.
             Could I have until [3–5 business days] to review it?"

This is always granted. It gives you time to:
  - Evaluate the full package
  - Get competing offers in
  - Research equity details
  - Prepare your counter
```

### Evaluate the full package — not just base salary

```text
Base salary        the monthly/annual cash — most predictable
Annual bonus       % of base, often discretionary, not guaranteed
Signing bonus      one-time, often with clawback if you leave within 1–2 years
Equity             RSUs vs options (see section below) — can be huge or worthless
Benefits           health insurance quality, 401k match, dental/vision
PTO                unlimited PTO ≠ more PTO (often means less — no payout at exit)
Remote policy      affects cost of living calculation significantly
Growth trajectory  promotion timeline, comp bands at next level
```

### Total Compensation (TC) calculation

```text
TC = Base + (Annual Bonus × probability) + (Annual Equity Vesting)

Example:
  Base:        $160,000
  Bonus:       15% target = $24,000 (assume 80% hit rate = $19,200 expected)
  RSU grant:   $200,000 over 4 years = $50,000/year
  TC:          $160,000 + $19,200 + $50,000 = $229,200

Compare TCs across offers, not just base salary.
A lower base with large equity can easily be worth more.
```

## Equity Deep Dive

### RSUs (Restricted Stock Units) — most common at public companies

```text
Company grants X shares that vest over a schedule (typically 4 years, 1-year cliff).
Value = number of shares × current stock price at vesting.
Tax: taxed as ordinary income at vesting (not at grant).
Risk: stock price can go down — your $200k grant may be worth $120k or $300k.

Vesting schedule example:
  Year 1: 25% (the cliff — you get nothing if you leave before this)
  Years 2–4: monthly or quarterly vesting of remaining 75%

Refresh grants: good companies give additional RSU grants each year
               (compensation for staying — ask about refresh policy)
```

### Stock Options — common at pre-IPO/startups

```text
Right to purchase shares at a strike price (exercise price = price at grant).
Value only if stock price > strike price.

Types:
  ISO (Incentive Stock Options): favorable tax treatment, only for employees,
       $100k/year limit, AMT risk if you exercise and hold
  NSO (Non-qualified Stock Options): taxed as ordinary income on spread at exercise,
       can be granted to contractors/advisors

Exercise window: typically 90 days after leaving (or you forfeit)
                 Some companies now offer 5–10 year windows — much better
Late-stage startup: options may have 409A valuation close to preferred price
                    — the "paper value" vs likely exit value can differ greatly

Questions to ask:
  - What is the current 409A valuation (fair market value)?
  - What was the last preferred price (from last funding round)?
  - What's the total fully diluted share count?
  - What is the liquidation preference stack? (affects payout in acquisition)
  - Is there a planned IPO or exit timeline?
```

### Evaluating startup equity honestly

```text
Expected value = (grant value at 409A) × (probability of liquidity event) × (dilution factor)

Rough heuristics:
  Series A:   ~5–10% chance of meaningful exit (high risk, high upside)
  Series B/C: ~15–25% chance
  Series D+:  ~30–50% chance (still uncertain)
  Pre-IPO:    ~60–80% chance of IPO, but upside is more limited

Ask: "If I were to exercise today, what would I pay and what's the FMV?"
     The spread = current value. If strike price ≈ 409A, the options have minimal value today.
```

## The Counter-Offer

### Always counter (unless offer exceeds your target by a lot)

```text
Counter 10–20% above the offer on the component you care most about.
Most companies have 10–15% room. Some have more.
Give a reason — it makes the counter feel justified, not greedy.
```

### Counter-offer script

```text
"Thank you so much for the offer — I'm genuinely excited about the role
 and the team. After reviewing it carefully, I was hoping we could get
 closer to [X] on base salary. Based on my research on similar roles
 in [city/remote] and my [specific experience/skills], I believe [X]
 better reflects the value I'd bring. Is that something we could work toward?"

Key elements:
  ✅ Express genuine enthusiasm first (you want them to feel good)
  ✅ Give a specific number (not a range — ranges get rounded to the bottom)
  ✅ Anchor with a reason (market data, your skills/experience)
  ✅ Ask as a question (collaborative, not adversarial)
  ❌ Never apologise for negotiating
  ❌ Never give ultimatums ("I need this or I'm walking")
  ❌ Never lie about competing offers
```

### What to counter on (in order of typical flexibility)

```text
1. Signing bonus    — often most flexible (doesn't affect salary bands long-term)
2. Base salary      — affects all future raises, bonuses, and comp at next job
3. Equity grant     — harder at large companies (band-constrained), easier at startups
4. Start date       — easy to negotiate, can be worth $$$ (more time to vest current equity)
5. Title            — affects future job hunting and comp at next role
6. Remote policy    — can be huge cost-of-living impact
```

### If they say "this is our best offer"

```text
They almost always have room. This is a negotiating tactic.

Response: "I appreciate that — and I understand there are constraints.
           Even if the base is fixed, is there flexibility on the signing
           bonus or equity grant? I want to make this work."

Or: "I understand. Could you give me a day to think it over?"
    (Silence often prompts them to improve the offer without you asking again)
```

## Competing Offers — The Leverage Play

```text
"I've received an offer from [Company X] for [TC]. This role is my first
 choice, but I'd need the total comp to be more competitive. Is there
 any flexibility to get closer to [number]?"

Rules:
  ✅ Only say this if you have the other offer in hand (or very close)
  ✅ Name the competitor — it makes it credible
  ✅ Say this role is your first choice (they need to feel it's winnable)
  ✅ Give a specific number to reach
  ❌ Don't use a lowball offer as leverage — it can backfire if they check
```

## When to Stop Negotiating

```text
You've gotten 2 rounds of back-and-forth and they haven't moved.
They've explicitly said the band is capped.
The relationship is starting to feel strained.
You've gotten what you needed (your target, not just above floor).

At that point: "Thank you for working with me on this — I'm excited to
               accept and join the team."

Then get it in writing (offer letter) before giving notice at current job.
```

## After the Offer — Before You Sign

```text
✅ Get the full offer in writing (email is fine, formal letter is better)
✅ Confirm: base, bonus target %, equity (shares + vesting), benefits, start date
✅ Read the offer letter — especially:
     - Clawback clauses on signing bonus (must repay if you leave within N months)
     - Non-compete (enforceability varies by state — consult a lawyer if broad)
     - Arbitration clause (you're waiving your right to sue in court)
     - IP assignment (what you build belongs to them — standard, but check scope)
✅ Give notice professionally — your reputation follows you
✅ Don't share your new salary with current employer — you'll get a counter-offer
   designed to keep you, not to value you (decide if you actually want to stay)
```

## Counter-Offer from Current Employer

```text
When you resign, your employer may counter-offer. Statistics consistently show:

- 80% of people who accept a counter-offer leave within 6 months anyway
- The underlying reasons you wanted to leave don't change
- Your loyalty is now questioned — you may be first on the layoff list

Ask yourself: "If they could pay me this, why weren't they already?"

When to consider staying:
  - The new offer solves a problem you didn't know was solvable (remote, title, scope)
  - You genuinely prefer staying and the counter closes the gap
  - The new company had a yellow flag you're rationalising away

Default: decline the counter-offer gracefully and leave on good terms.
```

## Useful Numbers to Know Before Negotiating

```text
Research these for your target role/level/location:
  - Median TC for Senior SWE in [city]: levels.fyi
  - 75th percentile (what top performers get): your target
  - Comp at next level: negotiate knowing where you want to be in 2 years
  - Vesting cliff date at current company: don't leave 2 months before it

Rule of thumb for job changes:
  Typical raise by staying:    3–5%/year
  Typical raise by switching:  15–30%+ (why switching is the fastest way to grow comp)
  Negotiating on a new offer:  typically adds 5–15% more on top of the initial offer
```
