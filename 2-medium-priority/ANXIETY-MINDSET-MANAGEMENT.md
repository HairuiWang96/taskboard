# Anxiety and Mindset Management

Interview nerves are real. They affect memory retrieval, language fluency, and the ability to think clearly. This is amplified when you're interviewing in a second language. The goal isn't to eliminate nerves — it's to get them low enough that your preparation can show through.

---

## Why Nerves Happen and Why That's Useful Information

Anxiety before interviews is your nervous system treating the situation as a threat. Your body prepares to fight or flee — heart rate increases, breathing shortens, the prefrontal cortex (where complex reasoning lives) gets less blood flow. This is why you blank on things you absolutely know when nervous.

The useful reframe: anxiety and excitement are physiologically identical. The only difference is interpretation. You can actually shift performance by changing how you label the feeling.

Unhelpful: "I'm so nervous, I'm going to mess this up"
Better: "I'm activated. My body is preparing me to perform."

This sounds corny but is supported by research (Alison Wood Brooks, Harvard Business School, 2014 — "reappraisal" works, "calm down" doesn't).

## Before the Interview

### The night before
- Do not cram new material. Whatever you don't know by now will not stick under pressure.
- Review one or two things you know well — this builds confidence, not last-minute knowledge.
- Prepare the logistics: link/location, who you're meeting, time zone, equipment check.
- Sleep. Cognitive performance degrades measurably with less than 7 hours. This is not optional.

### The morning of
- Eat. Low blood sugar impairs focus.
- Physical movement — even 10 minutes of walking raises your baseline mood and reduces anxiety.
- Avoid excessive caffeine. It amplifies physical anxiety symptoms (racing heart, trembling hands).
- Review your STAR stories or one technical topic you're confident in — prime the pump, don't study.

### 5 minutes before
**Physiological sigh** (fastest known method to reduce acute anxiety):
1. Inhale fully through your nose
2. At the top of the inhale, take a second short sniff to fully inflate lungs
3. Slow, long exhale through the mouth (longer than the inhale)
4. Repeat 2–3 times

This activates the parasympathetic nervous system. It works even if you're mid-anxiety spiral.

**Box breathing** (if you have more time):
- Inhale 4 seconds
- Hold 4 seconds
- Exhale 4 seconds
- Hold 4 seconds
- Repeat 4 cycles

## During the Interview

### When you blank on something you know

This happens to everyone, including candidates who get offers. The mistake is to freeze silently.

What to say:
- "I know this — let me think for a moment." (then actually think out loud)
- "I'm drawing a blank on the exact syntax, but here's the concept..." (then explain the concept)
- "I'd normally look that up, but I can walk you through how I'd approach finding it."

Narrating your thought process while you're stuck does three things:
1. Gives the interviewer something to evaluate (your reasoning, not just your memory)
2. Keeps the conversation active
3. Often triggers recall — talking through the problem unsticks your memory

Silence for more than 30–40 seconds is where interviews derail. Say something.

### When English fails you mid-sentence

In a second language, anxiety causes vocabulary retrieval to break down. What to do:
- Pause, breathe, restart the sentence (don't push through a tangled sentence)
- Use simpler words — clarity beats sophistication
- Say "Let me rephrase that" and try again
- It is okay to say "I'm not sure of the exact term in English — the concept is..."

Interviewers who are not native English speakers themselves will understand. Interviewers at international companies expect this. The mistake is to stop talking, not to speak imperfectly.

### When you don't know the answer at all

The worst thing you can do is pretend to know and talk in circles. Interviewers can tell. It wastes time and signals dishonesty.

Better:
1. Be direct: "I haven't worked with that specific API — but here's how I'd think about it..."
2. Show adjacent knowledge: "I know how [related thing] works, so I'd expect it to be similar in that..."
3. Explain your learning approach: "I'd read the docs, look for official examples, and probably set up a small test..."

Saying "I don't know, but here's how I'd find out" is an acceptable answer at senior level. It shows self-awareness.

### When a question is harder than expected

Don't rush. The interviewer is evaluating your process as much as your answer.

Script for buying time:
- "Let me make sure I understand the problem..." (repeat it back in your own words)
- "Before I jump in — can I ask a clarifying question?"
- "I want to think through the trade-offs here..." (then think out loud)

Rushing to a wrong answer looks worse than thinking carefully toward a good one.

### Resetting mid-interview when it goes wrong

Sometimes a question goes badly. You know it. The interviewer probably knows it. The mistake is to let it affect the rest of the interview.

Mental reset technique:
- Acknowledge to yourself: "That question was hard. It's done."
- The next question starts fresh — interviewers evaluate questions independently, not as a running score.
- If you bombed one section: "I think I could have done better on that — let me try to be clearer here."

One bad question rarely fails an interview. Letting one bad question derail the next three does.

## Reframing Rejection

Rejection is the default state of job searching. Even strong candidates get rejected at a 70–90% rate. This is structural, not personal.

Reasons candidates get rejected that have nothing to do with quality:
- The role was filled internally before the process ended
- Budget was cut mid-process
- Another candidate had a specific niche skill (e.g. "must have payment systems experience")
- The interviewer had a bad day and gave a lower signal score
- The team dynamic already had 4 introverts and they wanted energy

**What rejection tells you:** sometimes nothing. What it's good for is the debrief (what could you improve?), not as a referendum on your worth or ability.

After a rejection:
1. Fill in the debrief template within 24 hours
2. One day to feel it (this is normal and healthy)
3. Identify one thing to work on from the debrief
4. Move to the next application

The people who get the job are not necessarily the best engineers — they are the ones who kept applying.

## Second Language Specific

### Before the interview
- Warm up your spoken English the morning of. Talk through a STAR story or a technical concept for 5 minutes. Switching languages cold is harder.
- Record yourself explaining a concept you know well. Listen back. Identify habits (filler words, speed, clarity).

### During the interview
- Slow down deliberately. Non-native speakers often speak too fast when nervous. Slower speech sounds more confident, not less.
- Short sentences over complex grammar. "I designed the API. It handles authentication with JWT." beats a complex sentence you can't finish.
- If they misunderstood you: "Let me clarify — what I meant was..." (don't apologise, just clarify)

### Phrases that buy time and sound professional
- "That's a good question — let me think through it..."
- "I want to make sure I'm answering what you're actually asking — are you asking about X or Y?"
- "Let me approach this step by step..."
- "In my experience, the approach that works well is..."
- "I don't have direct experience with that specific tool, but I've worked with [related thing]..."

## Long-Term: Making Nerves Smaller

Nerves shrink with exposure. Every interview — practice or real — is making the situation less unfamiliar.

What compounds:
- Mock interviews with another person (not solo practice — stakes matter)
- Recording yourself and watching it back (uncomfortable, very useful)
- Doing first-round screens even for companies you're not excited about — low-stakes practice
- The debrief template — nerves come partly from uncertainty about what went wrong; tracking patterns removes that uncertainty

What doesn't help:
- Avoiding interviews until you feel ready (the feeling doesn't come without reps)
- Reading about interview technique without practicing it
- Telling yourself to "just be confident" without addressing the root cause

## Quick Reference

**Night before:** no cramming, review what you know, sleep  
**Morning of:** eat, move, light review of strong material

**When English fails:** pause, restart with simpler words  
**When you don't know:** admit it, show your reasoning approach  
**When a question goes badly:** reset, next question is fresh
